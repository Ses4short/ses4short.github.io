Research

Mechanistic models and interpretable machine learning for health and ecological systems.

Ecological Modelling & Climate Adaptation

My PhD thesis at York University focuses on understanding how interactions between industrial fishing, climate velocity (the rate at which climate zones shift), and marine fish distribution shape ecosystem dynamics and community livelihoods. I develop stage-structured mechanistic ODE models — fitted to Canadian stock assessment data — to estimate maturation, mortality, and recruitment parameters for economically significant species including Atlantic cod, Pacific halibut, and sockeye salmon.

A complementary strand of this work uses systematic review and predictive modelling to anticipate how fish and fishing communities will adapt to rapid climate velocities in Canadian waters, with implications for fisheries management policy.

Explainable Machine Learning (XAI)

In my data science PhD at Prince of Songkla University, I developed a lattice-theoretic framework for explainable machine learning — specifically, using Formal Concept Analysis (FCA) to provide structural, hierarchical explanations for ensemble methods including XGBoost and Gradient Boosting. The framework produces concept lattices that organise feature interactions in a transparent, auditable way, going beyond the local approximations typical of LIME and SHAP.

I applied this framework to the oil palm industry: predicting palm fruit oil volume and ripeness from spectral and physical features, and characterising the model's decision logic through FCA-inspired metrics.

Epidemiological Modelling

Across multiple collaborations, I have developed and analysed compartmental epidemic models for rotavirus (incorporating breastfeeding and vaccination dynamics), COVID-19 (Bangkok and Thai provincial contexts, including risk-stratified populations), HFMD (northern Thailand), and mpox (Thailand, limited-data forecasting). My modelling work spans analytical methods (Differential Transformation Method), numerical simulation, and feature-selection approaches applied to surveillance data.

Social Media & Sentiment Analysis

A recent collaboration applies NLP methods — sentiment analysis and latent Dirichlet allocation topic modelling — to Reddit and Facebook content from Canadian fishing communities, characterising attitudes toward species range shifts as climate change reshapes Canadian fisheries. This work bridges computational social science and fisheries management.
